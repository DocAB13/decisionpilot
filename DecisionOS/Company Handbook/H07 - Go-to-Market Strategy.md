## Preamble

A great product without a go-to-market strategy reaches no one. A strong go-to-market strategy built on a weak product creates short-term noise and long-term damage. H07 assumes that both the product and the market strategy must be honest reflections of each other.

This document defines how DecisionOS reaches its first users, converts them into paying customers, and builds the foundation for sustainable growth — without compromising the principles defined in H02 or the trust that H01 identifies as the core of the product.

H07 is not a marketing playbook. It does not define ad copy, visual identity, or social media calendars. It defines market positioning, acquisition channels, conversion strategy, revenue model, and success metrics. It is the authoritative reference for all commercial decisions made at DecisionOS.

Before reading H07, readers should be familiar with:
- **H01** — the target users, mission, and competitive positioning
- **H02** — the principles that constrain how we grow; **H01**'s Core Principles list includes the specific one (Principle 6: "Trust is the foundation of the product")
- **H06** — the subscription tiers and their feature definitions

## Part 1 — Market Positioning

### The Position We Own

DecisionOS occupies a position that no current product holds: **structured, personalized, AI-powered decision support for individuals facing choices that matter.**

This position is defined by what it is not as much as by what it is.

It is not general-purpose AI. ChatGPT can discuss any topic but is optimized for none. DecisionOS is optimized for one thing: helping a real person make a specific decision well.

It is not a comparison engine. Check24, MoneySuperMarket, and Finder aggregate options and surface tables. They are built for affiliate clicks, not for outcomes. They cannot ask a follow-up question. They do not know who you are.

It is not a search engine with AI synthesis. Perplexity finds and summarizes what exists. It does not reason about what fits your specific situation.

The position DecisionOS owns is the space between information and confidence — the gap that all three categories of existing tools leave unfilled.

### Positioning Statement

*For individuals facing important life decisions — financial, technological, personal, or professional — DecisionOS is the structured decision engine that transforms uncertainty into confident action. Unlike general AI assistants that answer questions and comparison websites that display tables, DecisionOS guides you through a complete decision process, remembers what you decide, and makes your next decision better.*

### Competitive Moat

The competitive moat of DecisionOS is not the AI. AI models are commodities. The moat is:

**The Decision Object.** A structured, persistent record of a complete decision — from context to lessons learned. This is something no competitor builds or stores. Every completed Decision Object makes the user more capable and the system more intelligent.

**Decision Intelligence over time.** A user who has made ten Decisions with DecisionOS makes their eleventh better. No competitor accumulates this kind of structured, outcome-grounded knowledge on behalf of their users.

**Trust built through transparency.** We show our reasoning. We surface risks. We tell you when we are uncertain. In a market where every product claims to be "AI-powered," transparency is the rarest and most defensible differentiator.

## Part 2 — Target Market

### Primary Market — MVP

The primary market for MVP is individual consumers in English-speaking and major European markets who face significant personal decisions in the following high-frequency, high-stakes categories:

**Financial decisions** — mortgages, personal loans, credit cards, savings products, investment platforms. These are decisions with large financial consequences and a high anxiety coefficient. The quality of the decision materially affects the user's life for years.

**Technology purchases** — smartphones, laptops, TVs, home appliances, software subscriptions. These are high-frequency decisions where the option space is overwhelming and the information is dense, technical, and biased by affiliate relationships.

**Insurance and protection** — health insurance, car insurance, home insurance, travel insurance. These are decisions most people defer, make once, and regret not thinking through more carefully.

**Career and education** — job offers, course selection, university programs, career transitions. These are decisions with long-horizon consequences and no obvious "right answer" framework.

**Travel and lifestyle** — hotels, airlines, holiday destinations, car rentals. High frequency, moderate stakes, high overlap with existing comparison tools — but our personalization advantage is decisive.

### Secondary Market — Post-MVP

**Financial advisors and consultants** who want a structured framework to guide clients through decisions — and who need a tool that documents the reasoning, not just the outcome.

**HR and career coaches** who guide individuals through career transition decisions and want AI-backed structure.

**Organizations** — banks, insurers, healthcare providers — who want to embed structured decision support into their customer journeys. This is Layer 3 of the long-term vision defined in H01, and it is not an MVP concern.

### Market Sizing

The total addressable market is every individual who makes a significant decision — which is every person on earth. That framing is accurate but useless for prioritization.

The serviceable addressable market for MVP is English-speaking adults (US, UK, Australia, Canada, Ireland) and major European markets (Germany, France, Spain, Romania, Netherlands) who have made at least one significant financial or technology purchase decision in the last 12 months and have used an online tool or comparison website to research it.

This population is in the hundreds of millions. DecisionOS does not need to capture a large share of this market to build a sustainable business. It needs to capture a small, highly satisfied share — users who trust the product, complete the full Decision Lifecycle, and return for their next decision.

## Part 3 — Acquisition Strategy

The acquisition strategy for DecisionOS is built around three channels, sequenced by phase.

### Phase 1 — Organic Search (Launch to Month 6)

**Why it comes first:** Organic search is the only acquisition channel that aligns perfectly with the moment of need. A person searching "should I choose mortgage A or mortgage B" or "how do I decide between iPhone and Android" is in exactly the state DecisionOS is built to serve. They are already in a decision. They are actively looking for help. The only question is whether DecisionOS appears when they search.

**What this requires:** Content that serves the decision-maker at the moment of search. Not generic articles about mortgages. Decision-specific guides: "How to choose between a fixed and variable mortgage rate when your income is irregular," "Deciding between two job offers: a framework." These guides demonstrate the DecisionOS approach, build search authority, and convert readers who want to apply the same structure to their own decision.

**Current state:** The project has existing SEO infrastructure — 17 guide pages, Google Search Console verification, GA4 integration, and a sitemap. These are the foundation. H07 does not rebuild this; it extends it with decision-specific content at higher depth.

**Target:** 50 organic sessions per day by Month 3. 500 per day by Month 6. These are conservative targets built for a solo operation, not for a funded team.

### Phase 2 — Word of Mouth and Direct Referral (Month 3 onwards)

**Why it comes second:** Word of mouth cannot be manufactured. It is earned by product quality and the emotional experience of making a good decision. The strategy here is to create the conditions for word of mouth, not to orchestrate it.

**What this requires:** Users who complete the full Decision Lifecycle and feel genuinely helped. The Reflection and Lessons Learned components are not only product features — they are the moments where a user crystallizes their experience into something they want to share. A user who writes "I compared a fixed and variable mortgage, followed DecisionOS's recommendation, and saved €8,000 over five years" has a story worth sharing.

**Mechanics:** Post-completion, the product offers a simple, optional share mechanism: "Share your decision process" (not the content of the decision — privacy is paramount). A user can share a summary of the type of decision they made and the general approach, without sharing personal details.

**Target:** 15% of completed Decision Objects result in a share action or a direct referral by Month 6. This is a secondary metric, not a primary one.

### Phase 3 — Targeted Paid Acquisition (Month 6 onwards)

**Why it comes third:** Paid acquisition before product-market fit is burning money to discover that the product does not retain. The sequence is: build trust organically, understand which users convert and stay, then amplify that profile with paid spend.

**Channels considered:**
- Search advertising targeting high-intent decision queries ("best mortgage for freelancer," "which laptop should I buy for video editing")
- Retargeting users who visited the homepage or started a Decision Object but did not complete it
- Content amplification for high-performing organic guides

**What we will not do:** Broad awareness campaigns. Display advertising. Social media advertising that interrupts rather than serves. These channels are incompatible with the trust-first positioning and the specific moment-of-need nature of our acquisition model.

**Target:** CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) below €15 per paying subscriber. LTV (Lifetime Value) of a Pro subscriber at 12-month retention above €50. A 3:1 LTV:CAC ratio is the minimum threshold before paid spend is increased.

## Part 4 — Conversion Strategy

### The Freemium Conversion Model

DecisionOS uses a freemium model with a specific structural logic: the free tier delivers genuine value — a complete Decision Lifecycle — and the paid tiers deliver more of that value, not a fundamentally different product.

This is not standard freemium. Standard freemium restricts core functionality to drive upgrades. DecisionOS restricts depth and history, not the core decision experience. A free user who makes one decision per month and records the outcome gets everything they need. A Pro user who makes multiple decisions, wants full history, and relies on AI Chat gets significantly more.

The conversion event is not "the user runs out of free decisions." It is "the user wants more than the free tier delivers, at the moment they want it."

### Conversion Triggers

Three moments drive the highest conversion probability:

**Trigger 1 — Post-Recommendation, before Final Decision.** The user has just received a Recommendation for a significant decision — mortgage, career, major purchase. This is the moment of highest perceived value. A non-intrusive prompt to create an account (to save this Decision) converts well here. The upgrade prompt is not shown — only account creation. Upgrade comes later.

**Trigger 2 — Decision History limit reached.** A Free user who has completed more than ten Decision Objects and tries to access their eleventh oldest is shown an upgrade prompt. By this point, the user has demonstrated retention and product value belief. The upgrade is positioned as "unlock your full decision history."

**Trigger 3 — AI Chat attempted on Free tier.** A user who has completed a Decision and wants to explore the Recommendation in depth through Chat is shown an upgrade prompt to Pro. This trigger has high intent — the user already trusts the Recommendation and wants to go deeper.

### Conversion Flow Principles

Per BR-06 in H06: upgrade prompts never interrupt a Decision flow. They appear only when a user explicitly attempts to use a tier-restricted feature.

Upgrade prompts must state clearly: what the feature is, what plan unlocks it, and what the plan costs. No dark patterns. No countdown timers. No artificial urgency.

After payment, the user returns to the exact context where they initiated the upgrade. The first experience of the newly unlocked feature must be immediate — zero friction post-payment.

### Pricing Rationale

**Free** — €0. Genuinely useful. The free tier is the product's primary acquisition channel — a user who gets real value from one Decision is the most likely referrer and future paying customer.

**Pro — €4.99/month.** Priced at the threshold of "I'll try it for a month" impulse decision for a middle-income user in Western Europe or North America. Below this price, the user does not feel they have made a real commitment. Above €7, the comparison to other subscriptions becomes unfavorable.

**Premium — €9.99/month.** Priced for power users and for users making high-stakes decisions (mortgage, career, investment) where the cost of the subscription is trivially small relative to the decision value. The Premium tier must deliver features that a user making a €300,000 mortgage decision would consider essential.

Annual pricing (20% discount) is a post-MVP feature. The monthly subscription is the correct starting point — it removes friction from the first payment and builds confidence before asking for an annual commitment.

## Part 5 — Retention Strategy

Acquiring a user is worthless if they do not return. In DecisionOS, retention is directly connected to the Decision Lifecycle. A user who completes a full Decision — including Outcome and Reflection — is significantly more likely to return than a user who received a Recommendation and never recorded what happened.

### Retention Mechanics

**The completed Decision loop.** Every user who records an Outcome has closed a loop and experienced the full value of the product. These users have a much higher probability of returning for their next decision. The most important retention metric is the rate of Outcome completion, not monthly active users.

**Decision History as a returning trigger.** A user who has three or more completed Decision Objects in their History has built a personal decision library. This library is a pull factor — something worth returning to, worth protecting, worth paying for. The longer and richer the History, the stronger the retention.

**Lessons Learned surfacing.** When a returning user starts a new Decision in a category where they have prior completed decisions, the system surfaces their own Lessons Learned. This creates a personalized experience that improves with use — the product becomes more valuable the more it is used, which is the strongest retention mechanic available.

**The 30-day Outcome prompt.** Per FR-10.1, the system prompts users to record an Outcome 30 days after a Decision is made. This prompt is not a notification (post-MVP) — it is a Dashboard prompt. It brings users back to the product with a specific, meaningful action to take. It is not engagement for its own sake. It is value completion.

### Retention Metrics

The following metrics define retention health for DecisionOS:

**Decision Completion Rate** — the percentage of started Decision Objects that reach Decision Made state. Target: 60% by Month 6.

**Outcome Recording Rate** — the percentage of Decision Made Decision Objects that receive a recorded Outcome. Target: 30% by Month 6. This is a lagging metric (30-day window) and is the most important signal of genuine product value.

**30-Day Return Rate** — the percentage of users who return within 30 days of completing a Decision Object. Target: 40% by Month 6.

**12-Month Pro Subscriber Retention** — the percentage of Pro subscribers who are still paying 12 months after first payment. Target: 60% by Month 12 of the product's life.

These targets are grounded in the nature of the product. DecisionOS is not a daily-use product. It is a high-value, episodic product used when a decision arises. Comparing its retention to daily-use apps is a category error. The correct comparison is to tools like Notion, 1Password, or legal software — products used intensely when needed, with strong retention because the cost of switching is high.

## Part 6 — Revenue Model

### Primary Revenue — Subscriptions

Subscription revenue is the primary and intended revenue model. It aligns the company's incentives with the user's outcomes. We are paid when users find the product valuable enough to continue using it — not when they click affiliate links.

**Revenue projection (conservative, solo operation):**

| Month | Paid Users | MRR (avg €6/user) |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | 10 | €60 |
| 6 | 50 | €300 |
| 12 | 200 | €1,200 |
| 18 | 500 | €3,000 |
| 24 | 1,500 | €9,000 |

These are not targets. They are the minimum viable commercial trajectory for a solo-operated product with organic growth only. They do not assume paid acquisition, partnership revenue, or viral growth.

### Secondary Revenue — Affiliate Partnerships

Affiliate revenue is a secondary channel that is acceptable only under strict conditions aligned with H02 Principle 7 (Human-Centered Design — personalization over popularity, no manipulation) and H02's Product Objective (data serves the user's own decisions, not third-party or company interests).

**Acceptable affiliate structure:** When a user completes a Decision and their Final Decision is a specific product or service, DecisionOS may include a link to that product or service that carries an affiliate tag. The link must be to the product the user chose — not to a sponsored alternative. The affiliate relationship must be disclosed.

**Not acceptable:** Affiliate relationships that influence Recommendations. Sponsored placements in the Recommendation View. Affiliate revenue from alternatives the user did not choose. Any affiliate structure where the revenue model conflicts with the Recommendation logic.

**Current affiliate infrastructure:** Amazon Associates (ID: decisionpilot-20), CJ Affiliate, Awin. These are maintained as infrastructure and activated only for categories where the user's Final Decision maps to a purchasable product (technology, appliances, travel bookings).

**Revenue expectation:** Affiliate revenue is a single-digit percentage of total revenue at maturity. It is not a primary strategy. It is a passive complement that must never compromise the product's integrity.

### Future Revenue — API and Institutional

Per Layer 2 and Layer 3 of the long-term vision in H01:

**API licensing (post-MVP):** Professional users — advisors, coaches, consultants — who want to run the DecisionOS framework on behalf of clients. Priced per seat or per decision volume.

**Institutional embedding (post-MVP):** Organizations that embed DecisionOS into their customer journey. Banks using it for mortgage decisions. Healthcare providers using it for treatment path guidance. Priced on a contract basis. This is a significant revenue opportunity but requires product maturity and a dedicated sales motion. It is not an MVP concern.

## Part 7 — Launch Plan

### Pre-Launch (Current Phase)

The product currently exists and is live at decisionpilot.tech. Pre-launch work focuses on completing the MVP feature set defined in H06 and ensuring the Decision Lifecycle is fully functional end-to-end.

Pre-launch does not mean "build in stealth." It means "build with care before amplifying." The product is open, accessible, and indexable. Organic traffic that arrives before the formal launch is welcome and useful — it provides real user behavior data.

**Pre-launch completion criteria:**
- All P1 requirements from H06 are implemented and pass acceptance criteria
- At least one internal tester has completed the full Decision Lifecycle (Draft → Completed) without assistance
- Subscription payments process correctly in production (not test mode)
- Error handling for AI failure, session interruption, and invalid input is verified

### Launch (Month 1)

**What launch means for DecisionOS:** A deliberate, focused announcement to an audience capable of generating organic reach and credible early feedback. Not a press release to a general audience. Not a Product Hunt launch on day one.

**Launch sequence:**

*Week 1 — Soft launch to personal network.* Invite 10–20 trusted individuals to use the product and complete a full Decision. Collect qualitative feedback. Fix critical issues. No public announcement.

*Week 2 — Product Hunt launch.* Product Hunt is the correct channel for a solo-built AI productivity tool. The audience is technically literate, actively looking for new tools, and willing to try early-stage products. A top-10 Product Hunt placement generates 500–2,000 sessions in 24 hours. The goal is not to win the day. The goal is to collect 50 honest reviews and identify the two or three things users find most valuable.

*Week 3–4 — Content seeding.* Publish three to five long-form decision guides targeting high-volume, high-intent search queries. These are not blog posts about DecisionOS. They are guides about making specific decisions — mortgages, smartphones, career changes — that demonstrate the DecisionOS approach and link to the product at the natural moment of need.

*Month 1 target:* 200 registered users, 20 completed Decision Objects, 5 paying subscribers, one piece of external coverage (newsletter, blog, or podcast mention).

### Growth (Month 2–6)

**Content engine.** Publish two decision guides per week targeting long-tail search queries in the primary decision categories (Financial, Technology, Career, Travel). Each guide is structured around the DecisionOS framework — context, goal, constraints, alternatives, recommendation — and demonstrates what a good decision process looks like. The guide itself is the value; the product is the natural next step.

**Community presence.** Be present in communities where people discuss the decisions DecisionOS serves: personal finance forums, technology comparison communities, career transition groups. Not as a marketer. As a participant who occasionally references the product when it is genuinely relevant.

**Partnership with adjacent tools.** Identify tools that serve users in the consideration phase — budget trackers, career development platforms, productivity tools — and explore content partnerships or integrations. These are post-launch conversations, not pre-launch obligations.

**Target by Month 6:** 2,000 registered users, 50 paying subscribers, €300 MRR, 200 organic sessions per day.

## Part 8 — Success Metrics

Success metrics are organized into three tiers: product health, commercial health, and strategic health. They are not vanity metrics. Each one is connected to a specific business or product outcome.

### Product Health Metrics

| Metric | Definition | MVP Target (Month 6) |
|---|---|---|
| Decision Completion Rate | % of started Decisions reaching Decision Made | 60% |
| Outcome Recording Rate | % of Decision Made objects with an Outcome recorded | 30% |
| Recommendation Acceptance Rate | % of users whose Final Decision matches the Recommendation | 65% |
| 30-Day Return Rate | % of users who return within 30 days of completing a Decision | 40% |
| AI Analysis Failure Rate | % of analysis requests that fail | < 1% |
| Wizard Abandonment Rate | % of started Wizards abandoned before Alternatives step | < 30% |

### Commercial Health Metrics

| Metric | Definition | MVP Target (Month 6) |
|---|---|---|
| MRR | Monthly Recurring Revenue | €300 |
| Paying Users | Users with active Pro or Premium subscription | 50 |
| Free-to-Paid Conversion Rate | % of registered users who convert to paid | 3% |
| 3-Month Subscriber Retention | % of paying users still active after 3 months | 70% |
| CAC | Cost to acquire one paying subscriber (organic = €0, paid TBD) | < €15 |
| ARPU | Average Revenue Per User per month | > €5.50 |

### Strategic Health Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Organic Search Sessions | Sessions from organic search per day | 500/day by Month 6 |
| Completed Decision Objects | Total completed Decision Objects in the system | 200 by Month 6 |
| Net Promoter Score | Would users recommend DecisionOS to someone facing a decision? | > 40 by Month 6 |
| Decision Categories Used | Number of distinct categories with completed Decisions | > 10 by Month 6 |

### Metrics We Explicitly Do Not Optimize For

Per H02's Decision Rule ("Does this feature help users make better decisions?") and H01 Core Principle 6 (Trust is the foundation of the product):

- **Daily Active Users** — DecisionOS is not a daily-use product. DAU as a primary metric would incentivize engagement features that do not serve Decision quality.
- **Session Duration** — A longer session is not a better product. A shorter session that reaches a confident Final Decision faster is better.
- **Page Views** — Content depth, not breadth, is what converts. Optimizing for page views creates shallow content that does not serve the decision-maker.
- **Social Shares** — A Decision is a private matter. Optimizing for shares would create incentives to make the product more shareable at the expense of making it more private and trustworthy.

## Part 9 — Constraints and Principles

The go-to-market strategy is not exempt from the principles defined in H01 and H02. Two H02 principles and one H01 principle have specific implications for GTM decisions.

### H02 Principle 7 — Human-Centered Design (Personalization Over Popularity)

We will not publish "what most users decide" statistics, even if they would drive social proof. We will not surface "trending decisions" or "popular alternatives." These mechanics would undermine the personalization that is our primary competitive advantage — and would be exactly the kind of nudging toward a specific choice Principle 7 rules out.

### H01 Core Principle 6 — Trust Is the Foundation of the Product (Build for Trust Before Growth)

We will not launch broad marketing campaigns before the product is ready to fulfill the trust it implies. A user who arrives at DecisionOS because of a marketing claim and receives a disappointing experience is a cost, not an acquisition. The sequence is always: trust first, then scale.

### H02's Product Objective — Data Serves the User, Not the Company

We will not use Decision Object data for advertising targeting, even anonymized aggregate data. We will not sell behavioral insights derived from user decisions. We will not build acquisition models based on personal information users shared in the context of a decision.

## Part 10 — What Success Looks Like at 24 Months

At 24 months from launch, DecisionOS has succeeded if:

- 1,500 paying subscribers trust it with decisions that matter to them
- The average paying subscriber has completed more than three Decision Objects and recorded at least two Outcomes
- At least one user has told us that a DecisionOS Recommendation changed the outcome of a decision worth more than €10,000 to them
- The product has a category-specific reputation in at least two decision domains (e.g., "the tool people use for mortgage decisions" or "the framework for evaluating job offers")
- MRR is growing consistently without paid acquisition
- The Decision Intelligence in the system — the accumulated Lessons Learned and Outcome data — is beginning to visibly improve Recommendation quality compared to launch

These are not financial targets. They are evidence of product-market fit. Financial targets follow from product-market fit — not the other way around.
